## Findings

### 1. Shorthand Systems: Pitman, Gregg, Teeline

**Compression mechanism.** Shorthand replaces alphabetic letters with phonemic strokes, abbreviated outlines, and brief forms (single strokes for high-frequency words like "the," "and," "is"). This reduces the physical writing unit count per word to roughly 1–3 strokes versus 4–8 strokes in longhand.

**Speed ratios.** Average longhand runs 25–45 WPM [1][22]. Professional shorthand reaches 120–180 WPM, implying a throughput ratio of roughly 4–6x at trained professional level [1][5]. Pitman's absolute record (Nathan Behrin, 1922, two-minute test) was 350 WPM — a 10x ratio against longhand [1]. Court reporters qualifying at 200–225 WPM represent a sustained ~6–8x ratio [22].

Across the three systems:
- **Pitman** (1837, phonetic): most phonetically rigorous; uses line thickness (thick vs. thin strokes) and position on the page to encode vowels — this enables highest theoretical speed but requires parallel attentional tracking of stroke weight. Professional range: 120–200 WPM.
- **Gregg** (1888, cursive phonetic): cursive flow removes the thick/thin constraint; documented at 280 WPM; designed for business stenographers targeting 100–120 WPM [3].
- **Teeline** (1968, alphabetic): strips to essential letters and uses word groups; fastest to learn (~6 weeks theory) but ceiling around 150 WPM [2].

**Learning cost.** Basics in 3–6 months; professional proficiency (100+ WPM) in 6–12 months; court-reporter speed (200+ WPM) in 2–4 years [5]. NCTJ journalism qualification requires 100 WPM Teeline and is embedded in a 19–40-week diploma program [4].

**Decoding cost.** Shorthand is personal and system-specific. Gregg at 280 WPM remains legible to other trained Gregg users [3]; Pitman and Teeline are similarly readable by trained practitioners of the same system. Ambiguity arises from omitted vowels (Pitman) or compressed word groups (Teeline); context resolves most ambiguity but transcription errors do occur, particularly for proper nouns.

**Audience.** Specialists only — journalists, court reporters, secretaries. General public cannot decode. Domain is near-universal within spoken-language capture: shorthand can record any spoken content but at the cost of learning a parallel symbol system.

### 2. Telegraphy Compression Codes

**Mechanism.** Commercial telegraph codes replaced entire phrases with a single codeword — typically a five-letter pronounceable string. Two five-letter codewords could be transmitted joined as a single ten-letter word, billing as one word, achieving an immediate ≥50% cost reduction per phrase replaced [7]. Bentley's Second Phrase Code, the ABC Telegraphic Code, the Western Union Universal Codebook (1907), and the Phillips Code (1879) all operated on this principle [7][8].

**Phillips Code specifically.** Compiled by Walter P. Phillips of the Associated Press, the 1879 edition covered ~3,000 high-frequency journalism terms and phrases, assigning each a 2–3 digit numeric code [6][21]. A veteran telegrapher's codification of collective operator practice, it became the newspaper industry standard through 1925 [6]. POTUS and SCOTUS entered the language through this code [6]. The compression ratio is estimable: a 5-word phrase like "President of the United States" collapses to one token. If average replaced phrases ran 4–8 words, compression is roughly 4:1 to 8:1 per coded phrase.

**Cost pressure.** Trans-Atlantic cable in 1866 cost $100 for 20 words [7]; per-word fees drove aggressive codebook adoption. By 1854, one-eighth of NYC–New Orleans telegrams were already in code [7]. These are the empirical adoption rates that imply cost savings were substantial — exact aggregate compression ratios over full messages are not reported in surviving literature, but per-phrase replacement rates of 5:1 to 10:1 are well-supported by specific examples (e.g., a single Bentley codeword representing "in leaving the dock struck the pier damaging the stern" — a 10-word phrase) [7].

**Decoding cost.** Requires the matching codebook at both ends. No codebook = unintelligible message. Codebook maintenance, edition-matching, and confidentiality were real operational costs. International regulations in 1903 restricted codewords to pronounceable strings in eight major languages to limit abuse [8].

**Audience.** Specialists (telegraphers, commercial users, newsrooms). General public sent plain telegrams. Domain was commercial and journalistic content — pre-defined phrase sets could not accommodate novel phrasing.

### 3. Military and Aviation Brevity Codes

**Mechanism.** Fixed-vocabulary single words (or short strings) encode tactical states that would otherwise require multi-word descriptions. NATO ACP-131 (current version ACP 131(F), April 2009) controls Q-codes and Z-codes [10]. Q-codes are authorized civilian and military; Z-codes military only [9]. The Multiservice Tactical Brevity Code covers air combat.

**Specific compression examples:**
- "Winchester" = aircraft out of air-to-air ordnance (not including gun if equipped) — 1 word replaces ~8 words [11].
- "Bingo" = minimum fuel state required for recovery to designated base — 1 word replaces ~9 words [11].
- "QRK" (Q-code) = "What is the readability of my signals?" — 1 code replaces a full sentence [9].
- A full missile/fuel inventory string "3-1-2 by 7 decimal 5" is far more compressed than "three AIM-120s, one AIM-7, two AIM-9s, gun with ammunition, 7,500 lbs fuel" [11].

This suggests per-term compression ratios of 6:1 to 10:1 for common tactical states.

**Why fixed vocabularies emerged.** Radio transmission under combat conditions requires zero disambiguation: every extra second of transmission extends exposure and increases probability of miscommunication under noise. ACP 131 was originally developed for Morse code CW operation [9] — minimizing transmitted characters had direct operational cost. The vocabulary is deliberately closed: brevity codes cannot handle novel tactical situations outside the codebook, requiring fallback to plain language.

**Decoding cost.** Lookup and memorization of the codebook. Military doctrine requires proficiency testing. Domain is strictly tactical/operational; no mechanism for creative or ambiguous content. This is the trade-off accepted in design: maximum brevity within a pre-defined domain, zero generality outside it.

**Audience.** Military specialists and aviation professionals. Q-codes have spilled into amateur radio (HAM operators), but Z-codes remain military-only [9][10].

### 4. Headlinese

**Mechanism.** Newspaper headlines systematically delete: articles (a, an, the), auxiliary verbs (is, was, has), the copula, and often pronouns and conjunctions. Verbs shift to simple present tense regardless of actual tense. Halliday (1967) described the result as "economy grammar" [14]. Nominalization packages entire clauses into compact noun forms.

**Compression ratio.** A full-prose sentence such as "The governor has signed the education funding bill into law" (10 words) typically becomes a headline like "Governor signs education bill" (4 words) — a roughly 2.5:1 word-count ratio. Research on optimal headline length converges on 6–11 words [12] versus full-article lead sentences averaging 25–35 words, suggesting a 3:1 to 5:1 compression ratio in word count terms. This is weaker than shorthand or telegraph codes but requires zero reader training.

**Decoding cost.** Near-zero for literate readers — the grammatical omissions are recoverable from world knowledge and context. The only decoding cost is the occasional ambiguity: "British Left Waffles on Falklands" is the canonical example of ambiguous head deletion. Academic studies (Mardh 1980, van Dijk 1988) identify systematic syntactic patterns that constrain ambiguity [14].

**Domain limits.** Headlinese works only for declarative news content. It cannot express nuance, conditionality, or extended argumentation — all of which require the grammatical machinery that headlinese deletes. Opinion, analysis, and technical domains are poorly served.

**Audience.** General literate public. No specialist training required.

### 5. SMS/Text-Speak and Twitter Constraint Effects

**Technical origin.** The 160-character SMS limit derives from GSM 03.38: 140 bytes × 8 bits = 1,120 bits ÷ 7 bits/char = 160 characters (GSM-7 encoding). Including non-Latin characters forces UCS-2, halving the limit to 70 characters [15].

**Compression strategies.** Two classes: (a) abbreviation/acronym (LOL, OMG, BRB); (b) vowel deletion, reading back from consonant strings ("dctnry" for "dictionary", "kybrd" for "keyboard") [15]. These achieve word-level compression of approximately 30–60% in character count for abbreviated terms. However, David Crystal's study found only ~10% of his own messages used SMS abbreviations; an American study found <20% of messages used SMS language at all [15]. This suggests character-limit pressure produced a modest, not universal, compression register.

**Twitter experiment.** When Twitter doubled the limit from 140 to 280 characters in November 2017, 9% of English tweets had previously hit the 140-char cap; only 1% hit the 280-char cap post-expansion [16]. This demonstrates that constraint was binding for a minority of users. Dutch language research found pre-expansion tweets had more textisms and abbreviations; after expansion, users wrote longer words and sentences [17]. The compression behavior was constraint-driven and reversed when constraint lifted.

**Decoding cost.** Moderate for older or less-immersed readers; low for peer groups who share the lexicon. No formal training required. Acronyms like LOL were widely adopted into mainstream usage and then into dictionaries — indicating successful diffusion outside the original constraint environment.

**Domain limits.** SMS/text-speak is adequate for social, phatic, and informal coordination. It is inadequate for technical, legal, or formal register. The constraint also drove form-over-content choices: messages were shaped by character limits rather than communicative needs.

### 6. Pidgins and Creoles (Tok Pisin, Hawaiian Creole English)

**Vocabulary compression.** Tok Pisin has a core vocabulary of approximately 2,500–3,000 words [18][19] against English's roughly 170,000+ documented words. The ratio is ~60:1 in vocabulary size. This is the most extreme vocabulary compression of any system in this survey. The mechanism is not abbreviation but semantic broadening: individual words cover larger conceptual territory, supplemented by circumlocution (e.g., "skru bilong lek" — literally "screw belonging to leg" — means "knee") [19].

**Grammar compression.** Tok Pisin lacks: noun case, noun number, noun gender, tense morphology (inflections), most bound morphology, and has only four prepositions ("long," "bilong," "wantaim," "olsem") covering all spatial/relational meaning [19]. Hawaiian Creole English (HCE) similarly reduces bound morphology versus Standard American English and uses a distinct, systematic tense-aspect-modality system; it emerged from first-generation children of plantation pidgin speakers in the late 1800s [20].

**Key distinction from other systems.** Pidgins and creoles are not designed compression systems — they are emergent outcomes of multilingual contact with communication necessity. The compression is in vocabulary and morphology, not in message-token count. A Tok Pisin speaker must use more words to convey concepts that English handles with a single lexeme; the per-word semantic load is higher but message token count is not necessarily lower.

**Compression ratio (qualified).** Vocabulary compression: ~60:1 (Tok Pisin vs. English word count). Message compression: not measurably lower, and often higher because circumlocution requires more tokens per concept. Grammar morphology compressed: dramatically — from ~200+ English inflectional and derivational morphemes in regular use to near-zero in Tok Pisin.

**Decoding cost.** For English speakers, Tok Pisin is partially intelligible immediately due to English lexical base; full fluency estimated at several months [18]. HCE is a native language for 600,000 Hawaiians — no decoding cost for those speakers, but it represents a social barrier for outsiders.

**Domain limits.** Tok Pisin has expanded from plantation pidgin to Papua New Guinea's official co-language and can now express political, legal, and technical content. Early-stage pidgins, however, are severely domain-limited to immediate practical coordination. Technical and abstract domains require either loanwords or extended circumlocution.

- Specific NATO brevity examples: Winchester, Bingo confirmed with definitions

**Uncertain (single-source or inferred):**

- Per-message character saving from SMS abbreviation strategies — not quantified as an aggregate ratio in available sources
- Tok Pisin early-stage domain limits versus contemporary expression range — the vocabulary count is confirmed, but the contemporary domain coverage claim (now covers technical/political) relies on descriptive sources, not quantitative study
